}1b:T728,Illamurta Springs Conservation Reserve is one of the most remote and least visited reserves in Central Australia. Tucked away in the foothills of the James Range, southwest of Alice Springs, the reserve protects a series of natural springs that flow from the range and sustain life in this arid environment. These clear, spring-fed pools are shaded by river red gums and reeds – they are a vital water source for wildlife and were an important camping spot for Aboriginal people, as well as early pastoralists and police patrols. The springs are permanent or near-permanent, offering a green oasis where birds and animals concentrate. Notably, the reserve also contains the stone ruins of an Illamurta Springs Police Camp established in the 1890s. Visitors can see low stone wall remnants of the police station building and outposts; this station was once used as a base for police (Native and European) who patrolled the region during the tumultuous frontier era. Interpretive signs (if still present) give some historical context to the police camp and its role in administering law (often harshly) over local Aboriginal populations. Getting to Illamurta Springs requires a high-clearance 4WD and navigation of unmarked tracks – there are no facilities like picnic tables or defined walking trails. Those who do make the journey are rewarded with absolute tranquility, a chance to explore the small gorge and spring pools (look for waterbirds, frogs, perhaps even the shy black-footed rock-wallaby on nearby cliffs), and a connection with little-known history. Camping is permitted and you’re likely to have the entire reserve to yourself.
